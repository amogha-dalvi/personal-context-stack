# Conducting Customer Research — Full Workflow

## Prerequisites

Read `person_[name].md` and `icp_[name].md` from memory before starting. You need to know who this person is and who their audience is — this skill goes deeper into what that audience *thinks, feels, and does*.

## Setting the frame

> You've defined who you're for. Now I want to understand what's going on inside their head — not what you *want* them to think, but what they actually think before they ever find you.
>
> You've talked to these people, sold to them, served them, maybe even been them. That experience is the data. I'll ask questions — you tell me what you've seen.

---

## Vein 1: Worldview

What the audience believes *before* they encounter this person. Their mental model of the problem, what they've tried, what they think the answer is.

### Current beliefs

Questions to pull on:
- When your ideal customer thinks about [their problem domain], what do they believe is causing it?
- What's the story they tell themselves about why things aren't working?
- What do they think the solution looks like? (This is often wrong — that gap is where your value lives.)
- What have they already tried? What happened?

**Listen for:** the gap between what they believe and what's actually true. That gap is the entire basis for content that shifts perspective rather than just informing.

### Awareness level

Questions to pull on:
- When they find you, do they know they have the problem — or do you have to name it for them?
- Do they know solutions exist, or do they think this is just "how it is"?
- Are they comparing options, or are they still figuring out what to search for?

**Listen for:** where on the awareness spectrum the audience sits. This determines whether content should be problem-aware ("here's what's actually wrong"), solution-aware ("here's why this approach works"), or product-aware ("here's why this specific offer").

### Prior experiences

Questions to pull on:
- What have they been burned by? Bad consultants, courses that didn't work, tools that promised and underdelivered?
- What are they skeptical of because of past experience?
- Is there an industry norm or common approach they've tried and been disappointed by?

**Listen for:** scar tissue. The audience's past failures shape their objections, their language, and their trust threshold. Content that acknowledges what they've already tried (and why it didn't work) builds credibility faster than anything.

---

## Vein 2: Language & Objections

The exact words they use and the friction points that stop them from acting.

### Their language

Questions to pull on:
- When they describe their problem to a peer (not to you), what words do they use?
- What do they Google when they're looking for help?
- Is there a phrase they keep saying that you hear over and over?
- Do they use industry jargon or plain language? Which specific terms?

**Listen for:** the vocabulary gap. There's almost always a difference between how the person describes the problem (expert language) and how the audience describes it (felt language). Content needs to use the audience's words, not the expert's.

### Objections

Questions to pull on:
- When someone is interested but doesn't buy/act, what do they say?
- What's the most common "yeah but..." you hear?
- Is the hesitation about price, timing, trust, or something else?
- What do they need to believe before they'll move forward?
- Is there someone else they need to convince? (Boss, partner, team)

**Listen for:** the real objection vs. the stated one. "I need to think about it" is stated. "I'm not sure this will work for my specific situation" is real. The real objection is what content needs to address.

### What they need to hear

Questions to pull on:
- What's the thing that, when you say it, makes them go "yes, that's exactly it"?
- Is there a specific proof point or example that consistently tips people over?
- What question, when you answer it, makes the conversation shift?

**Listen for:** the unlock phrase. Almost everyone has one — the sentence or framing that collapses the audience's resistance. This becomes a core content asset.

## Vein 3: Buying Patterns

How they make decisions — not how you'd like them to, but how they actually do.

### Decision process

Questions to pull on:
- How long does it typically take from "interested" to "yes"?
- Do they decide alone or does someone else weigh in?
- What do they do between first hearing about you and taking action? (Research, ask peers, sit on it, compare?)
- Is there a season, cycle, or budget period that affects timing?

**Listen for:** the actual journey, which is rarely linear. Understanding whether they binge your content for a week then buy, or lurk for six months then DM, changes everything about content strategy.

### What triggers action

Questions to pull on:
- Think of someone who bought/hired you quickly — what was happening in their world?
- Is there a pain threshold where they go from "I should fix this" to "I'm fixing this now"?
- Does a specific event or deadline create urgency? (Funding round, board meeting, churn spike, new quarter)

**Listen for:** trigger events that are *external* to the person's content. You can't create urgency — but you can be present when urgency arrives. These triggers tell the content engine when to push conversion content.

### What they compare against

Questions to pull on:
- When they're considering you, what else are they looking at? (Competitors, DIY, doing nothing, hiring someone internal)
- What do they think is the alternative to working with you?
- Is "do nothing" the biggest competitor?

**Listen for:** the real competitive set, which is often not who the person thinks. "Do nothing" and "hire an internal person" are competitors too. Content needs to address the actual alternatives.

## Vein 4: Attention & Environment

Where the audience spends time, what they consume, who they trust, and what formats reach them.

### Where they are

Questions to pull on:
- Where do they spend professional time online? (LinkedIn, Twitter, Slack communities, Reddit, forums, newsletters)
- Are they in any paid communities, memberships, or cohorts?
- Do they attend events — conferences, meetups, webinars? Which ones?
- Where do they go when they need to learn something? (YouTube, podcasts, courses, books, Google)

**Listen for:** the *specific* places, not categories. "LinkedIn" is too broad. "They scroll LinkedIn at 7am and engage with tactical posts, not thought leadership" is useful.

### What they consume

Questions to pull on:
- Who do they already follow or listen to in this space?
- What content format do they actually engage with — long posts, short threads, podcasts, video, newsletters?
- Do they read or do they skim? Do they save things or act immediately?
- What kind of content makes them share or tag someone?

**Listen for:** format and depth preferences. Some audiences want 2,000-word breakdowns. Some want a single sharp insight in three lines. This directly shapes the content engine.

### Who they trust

Questions to pull on:
- Whose opinion do they respect in this space? (Not aspirational follows — people they'd actually take advice from)
- Do they trust peers more than experts, or experts more than peers?
- What kind of proof do they need — case studies, data, personal stories, credentials?
- Are there communities or groups where recommendations carry weight?

**Listen for:** the trust architecture. This determines whether content should lead with credibility ("I built this at X company") or relatability ("I was in your exact situation") or social proof ("here's what happened for a client").

## Synthesis template

After the conversation, organize into a research profile:

```markdown
# [Name]'s Customer Research — [ICP Segment Name]

## Worldview
- What they believe the problem is: [in their words]
- What's actually driving it: [the gap between their belief and reality]
- Awareness level: [unaware / problem-aware / solution-aware / product-aware]
- What they've tried: [prior solutions and why they failed]
- Scar tissue: [what they've been burned by — shapes trust threshold]

## Language
- How they describe the problem: [exact phrases, their words not yours]
- What they search for: [Google terms, questions they ask]
- Vocabulary gap: [difference between expert language and their language]

## Objections
- Stated objections: [what they say out loud]
- Real objections: [what's actually stopping them]
- What they need to believe: [preconditions for action]
- The unlock phrase: [the thing that collapses resistance]
- Other decision-makers: [who else needs convincing]

## Buying patterns
- Timeline: [how long from interest to action]
- Decision process: [solo, committee, peer-validated]
- Trigger events: [external events that create urgency]
- Competitive set: [what they're actually comparing against — including "do nothing"]

## Attention & environment
- Where they spend time: [specific platforms, communities, events]
- What they consume: [formats, depth, frequency]
- Who they trust: [names, types, proof preferences]
- What makes them share: [content triggers for amplification]
- Trust architecture: [credibility vs. relatability vs. social proof]
```

If tiered ICP exists, create a research section for each segment. Note where segments overlap and where they diverge.

## Validation

Two checks:

### The "would they recognize themselves" test
> If your ideal customer read this worldview and objections section — would they feel seen? Or would they think "that's kind of right but not exactly"?

If not exact — sharpen. Go back to their words, not your interpretation.

### The "does this change what you'd write" test
> Knowing this — does it change what content you'd create, how you'd frame your offer, or where you'd show up?

If nothing changes — the research isn't specific enough yet. Generic insights don't shift strategy.

## Common mistakes

- **Using your language instead of theirs.** "They need a retention-focused GTM strategy" is expert framing. "My users keep leaving and I've tried everything" is what they actually feel. The research should read like the audience talking, not the expert diagnosing.
- **Listing objections without the real one underneath.** "It's too expensive" is almost never the real objection. "I'm not sure it'll work for my situation" or "I can't justify this to my boss" — that's what content needs to address.
- **Treating attention as a channel list.** "LinkedIn, Twitter, podcasts" is useless. *How* they use those channels, *when*, and *what* they engage with there — that's what shapes content strategy.
- **Skipping the awareness level.** Content for someone who doesn't know they have a problem looks completely different from content for someone comparing solutions. This single field reshapes the entire content engine.
- **Guessing instead of mining.** Every answer should be traceable to something the person has actually experienced — a conversation, a sales call, a DM, a pattern they've noticed. If they're guessing, flag it and note the confidence level.
