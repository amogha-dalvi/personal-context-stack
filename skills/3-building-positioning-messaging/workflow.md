# Building Positioning & Messaging — Full Workflow

## Prerequisites

Read all three upstream files from memory before starting:
- `person_[name].md` — throughline, credibility, beliefs, thinking patterns
- `icp_[name].md` — who they serve, pain states, anti-persona
- `research_[name].md` — worldview, language, objections, buying patterns, attention environment

This skill *synthesizes* — it doesn't discover. If any of these files feel thin, flag it and consider re-running the upstream skill before proceeding.

## Setting the frame

> You've told me who you are, who you're for, and what's going on inside their head. Now I'm going to pull all of that into something sharp — a position, a message, and a voice. Think of it as: what territory do you own, what do you say to claim it, and how do you sound when you say it.
>
> I'll draft, you react. Easier to sharpen something that exists than to build from blank.

## Phase 1: Positioning

Positioning is the answer to: **what space do you own that no one else credibly can?**

It lives at the intersection of three things:
- What the person **believes and has built** (from person profile)
- What the audience **needs and can't find** (from ICP + research)
- What **no one else is saying** (the white space)

### Finding the intersection

Don't ask "what's your positioning?" — synthesize it from what you already know, then test it.

Step 1: Pull the raw inputs.

From person profile:
- Their sharpest beliefs (the ones with edge)
- Their throughline (the narrative spine)
- Their credibility stack (what gives them the right)

From ICP + research:
- The audience's core pain (in their words)
- The gap between what the audience believes and what's true
- What they've tried that didn't work
- What the competitive set looks like

Step 2: Draft the positioning statement.

Use this structure:

```
## Positioning

### Category
[The space they own — a specific phrase, not a job title. Not "marketing consultant" but "context engineering for GTM teams". This should be a category they can credibly claim and that the audience would recognize as relevant to their pain.]

### One-liner
[For [who], [what you do], [against what]. Example: "For healthtech companies losing patients after month 3 — a retention system built upstream in how you position and sell, not downstream in product fixes."]

### The core belief
[The single conviction that drives everything. This comes directly from the person's beliefs — the one that's both true and differentiated. Stated sharply.]

### What this is not
[Explicit negatives. Prevent drift. "Not a marketing agency. Not a growth hacker. Not someone who'll run your ads." These come from the anti-persona and from what the person rejects about their industry.]

### The right to claim this
[Why this person, specifically, owns this position. Traces back to throughline and credibility stack. Not "10 years experience" but "built three retention systems that reduced churn by 40% in healthtech — the theory comes from the practice."]
```

Step 3: Test with the user.

> Does this feel like territory you'd plant a flag in? Not whether it's perfect yet — but is this the *right space*? If not, what's off — is it too broad, too narrow, or just the wrong angle?

## Phase 2: Messaging

Messaging translates positioning into what you actually *say* — in the audience's language, calibrated to where they are in the awareness journey.

### Core narrative

The story that connects the person's position to the audience's pain. Structure:

```
## Core narrative

### The world as they see it
[What the audience currently believes — from their worldview in research. Start where they are, not where you want them to be.]

### What's actually happening
[The truth behind their pain — the gap between their belief and reality. This is where the person's expertise reframes the problem.]

### The shift
[What needs to change — the insight that moves them from their current belief to a new understanding. This is the person's core belief, stated in the audience's language.]

### What becomes possible
[The outcome — not features, not process, but what changes when they see it differently and act on it.]
```

This narrative is the backbone of all content. Every post, email, and sales page is a fragment of this story.

### Key messages by awareness level

From customer research, you know the audience's awareness level. Build messages for each stage they might be in:

```
### Messages by awareness level

**Unaware** (don't know they have the problem):
- Hook: [names the pain they feel but haven't diagnosed — uses their symptoms, not your diagnosis]
- Job: make them go "wait, is that what's happening?"

**Problem-aware** (know the problem, don't know the solution):
- Hook: [reframes the problem — challenges what they think is causing it]
- Job: shift their mental model so they see the real cause

**Solution-aware** (know solutions exist, comparing):
- Hook: [differentiates your approach — why this works when others didn't]
- Job: make them understand why your specific angle matters

**Product-aware** (know your offer, deciding):
- Hook: [addresses the real objection — from research, not the stated one]
- Job: collapse the last hesitation
```

### Proof points

Map credibility markers to objections:

```
### Proof points
- [Objection 1] → [specific proof that addresses it — built X, achieved Y, client Z experienced...]
- [Objection 2] → [proof point]
- [Objection 3] → [proof point]
```

These come from the person's credibility stack matched against the audience's real objections.

### The unlock phrase

From customer research — the sentence that collapses resistance. Formalize it:

```
### The unlock phrase
"[exact phrasing]"
- When to use: [which stage, which context]
- Why it works: [what belief it shifts]
```

## Phase 3: Voice

Voice is how the person sounds when they're at their best. It's not invented — it's extracted from how they already communicate when they're not overthinking it.

### Extraction process

If you have writing samples (posts, emails, threads), analyze them for:
- Sentence length patterns (short and punchy? long and flowing?)
- Paragraph structure (single-line paragraphs? dense blocks?)
- Punctuation habits (em dashes? parentheticals? ellipses?)
- Opening patterns (start with a question? a statement? a story?)
- Closing patterns (CTA? question? sharp last line?)
- Use of "I" vs. "we" vs. "you"
- How they handle transitions
- Whether they use metaphors/analogies and from what domains

If you don't have samples, pull from the conversation itself — how they talked during the interview skills is the best signal.

### Voice output

```
## Voice

### Tone
[2-3 words — not generic. Not "professional and warm." More like "direct, dry, grounded" or "sharp, personal, no-nonsense"]

### How they sound at their best
[1-2 sentences describing the *feel* — what reading their best content is like. "Reads like a smart friend explaining something over coffee — never lectures, never performs, treats the reader as an equal who just hasn't seen this particular angle yet."]

### Structural patterns
- Paragraph length: [short / medium / mixed]
- Sentence rhythm: [varies length or consistent]
- Opens with: [questions / statements / stories / data]
- Closes with: [CTA / question / sharp last line / callback]
- Punctuation: [em dashes, parentheticals, minimal, etc.]
- Person: [I / we / you-focused]

### Never say
[Specific banned words and phrases. Pull from: what the person explicitly rejects, what sounds like their industry's worst habits, what would make them cringe if they read it in their own content.]

Examples:
- No: [word/phrase] — Why: [what it signals that's wrong]
- No: [word/phrase] — Why: [what it signals that's wrong]

### Example of voice done right
[One real sentence they wrote or said that captures the voice perfectly. Annotate why it works.]

### Voice vs. competitors
[How this voice differs from the default in their space. "Most [industry] content sounds like [X]. This sounds like [Y] because [reason]."]
```

## Validation

Three checks:

### The "only you" test
> Could anyone else in your space say this exact thing with the same credibility? If yes — it's not differentiated enough.

### The "would you post this" test
> If I wrote a LinkedIn post using this positioning, this core narrative, and this voice — would it sound like you? Or would you rewrite it?

### The "does it exclude" test
> Does this positioning clearly signal to the wrong people that you're not for them? If everyone nods — it's too safe.

## Common mistakes

- **Positioning that describes a service, not a belief.** "I help companies with retention" is a service. "Retention is a GTM problem, not a product problem" is a position. The belief is what makes it ownable.
- **Messaging in expert language.** The core narrative should be in the audience's language. If the audience wouldn't use these words to describe their problem, the messaging won't land.
- **Voice by aspiration, not extraction.** Don't ask "how do you want to sound?" — analyze how they *actually* sound in their best moments. Aspirational voice guidelines produce content that feels performative.
- **Skipping the "never say" list.** The prohibitions are often more useful than the prescriptions. They prevent the most common drift into generic industry voice.
- **Making positioning too broad to be wrong.** Good positioning makes some people disagree. If it's safe enough that no one would push back, it's not specific enough to attract the right people either.
- **Disconnecting proof points from objections.** Proof points aren't a trophy case — they're answers to specific doubts. Map each one to the objection it addresses.
