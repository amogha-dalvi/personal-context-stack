# Alex Chen — Person Profile

## The throughline

Alex spent six years as a product manager at two healthtech startups, both times watching the same pattern: the product team would build retention features (better onboarding, engagement nudges, in-app education) while churn kept climbing. The third time, at a Series B company where she ran growth, she traced the churn back upstream — patients were dropping off because the product was sold to the wrong people with the wrong expectations. The fix wasn't a better product. It was a better GTM motion that set accurate expectations and attracted patients who'd actually stick.

She left to consult, built retention systems for four healthtech companies, and every time the biggest lever was upstream — positioning, messaging, onboarding framing. She now teaches this through a framework she calls RETAIN and runs cohort-based workshops for healthtech growth teams.

## What they've built

- Growth function at MedBridge Health (Series B) — reduced 90-day patient churn from 34% to 19% by restructuring the acquisition-to-onboarding narrative
- RETAIN framework — a 6-step system for diagnosing retention problems upstream in GTM rather than downstream in product
- Consulting engagements with 4 healthtech companies (2 digital health, 1 RPM, 1 telehealth) — all showed measurable churn reduction within 2 quarters
- A cohort-based workshop ("Retention by Design") now in its third cohort, 40+ alumni

## What they believe

- **Retention is a GTM problem, not a product problem.** Most healthtech companies try to fix churn with product features when the real issue is who they acquired and what those people expected. The product team can't fix what the marketing team broke.
- **Patient churn is a messaging failure.** If a patient drops off at month 3, the problem started at month 0 — in how the product was described, promised, and positioned.
- **Growth teams in healthtech copy B2B SaaS playbooks and it doesn't work.** The buying cycle, the emotional stakes, the compliance environment — all different. Healthtech needs its own retention logic.
- **The best retention metric is expectation accuracy.** If what a patient experiences matches what they were told to expect, they stay. The gap between promise and reality is the churn predictor.

## How they think

Traces problems upstream — when something breaks, her instinct is to go earlier in the chain, not deeper into the symptom. Uses "where did this actually start?" as a diagnostic question for everything.

Thinks in systems, not tactics. Doesn't trust isolated fixes — wants to see how a change propagates through the full journey.

Reaches for healthcare-specific analogies: "treating symptoms vs. treating causes" maps directly to how she sees retention work.

## What drives them

Watched patients lose access to care they needed because the company that served them couldn't retain enough users to stay funded. The business problem (churn) and the human problem (patients losing care) are the same problem to her. Fixing retention isn't just a growth metric — it's how digital health companies survive long enough to actually help people.
